5.1 Introduction

In the previous chapter we introduced a powerful framework for understanding clients. We learned to analyze their stage of life, the source of their wealth, their behavioral biases, and their personality types. We applied this framework to the Schmidt-Miller family, uncovering Klaus’s loss-averse conservatism, Elke’s emotional connection to her inherited portfolio, and Lukas’s overconfident risk-taking. We identified their goals, quantified their liquidity needs, assessed their risk tolerance, and calculated their return requirements. Now comes the task of translating the understanding of their finacial needs into an investment plan.

An Investment Policy Statement, or IPS, is the document that bridges the gap between client analysis and portfolio construction. It takes everything we have learned about the client and translates it into a clear, written framework that will guide every investment decision made on their behalf. The IPS shows where the portfolio is going, it documents the agreement between advisor and client about how to get there.

By the end of this chapter, you will understand not only what an Investment Policy Statement contains but also how to craft one that truly reflects the client’s complete financial needs. You will see how the concepts of client analysis translate into practical, actionable documents.

5.2 Firm-Level Investment Policy

Before we can write an Investment Policy Statement for an individual client, we must understand the context in which all such documents are created. Every investment firm operates according to a set of beliefs, principles, and practices that shape how it manages client portfolios. These may be formally documented in a firm-level investment policy or simply embedded in the firm’s culture. This firm-level policy provides the framework within which all client-specific IPS documents are developed.

5.2.1 Components of a Firm-Level Investment Policy

A firm-level investment policy describes the investment philosophy, strategic approach, and guiding principles that define how a firm manages client assets. It serves as the foundation upon which every client-specific Investment Policy Statement is built. While the specific content varies from firm to firm, most investment policies address several key elements.

5.2.1.1 Investment Philosophy

The investment philosophy expresses the firm’s beliefs about how financial markets work and how investment returns are generated. Does the firm believe that markets are generally efficient, meaning that it is difficult to consistently outperform through security selection? If so, it will likely favor passive investment strategies using low-cost index funds and exchange-traded funds. Does the firm believe that skilled investors can identify mispriced securities and generate excess returns? If so, it will dedicate resources to active management and manager research. Does the firm believe that certain factors, such as value, momentum, or quality, historically have delivered premium returns? If so, it may construct portfolios that tilt toward these factors.

These beliefs have profound implications for how client portfolios will be constructed and managed. A clear philosophy ensures that all investment decisions are made consistently and that clients understand what they are buying.

5.2.1.2 Asset Allocation Framework

The asset allocation framework provides guidelines for how client portfolios should be diversified across different asset classes. This typically includes the asset classes the firm considers suitable for client portfolios, such as global equities, fixed income, real estate, commodities, or alternative investments. It may also include strategic allocation ranges that guide how these asset classes are combined for clients with different risk profiles.

5.2.1.3 Risk Management Principles

Every firm must have a clear approach to managing the risks inherent in investing. The risk management principles section of the policy addresses how the firm identifies, measures, and mitigates market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk, and operational risk. This typically includes diversification requirements, such as limits on exposure to any single security, sector, or issuer. It may also include guidelines for the use of derivatives or other risk management tools and procedures for monitoring and responding to changing risk conditions.

5.2.1.4 Ethical and ESG Considerations

Increasingly, firms are incorporating environmental, social, and governance considerations into their investment policies. This section articulates the firm’s approach to ESG factors, whether through negative screening that excludes certain industries, positive screening that favors companies with strong practices, or full integration that considers ESG factors alongside traditional financial analysis. It may also address any firm-level commitments to responsible investing, such as signing the United Nations Principles for Responsible Investment.

5.2.2 Firm-Level Policy Example

To make these concepts concrete, let us consider the investment policy of Elara Fischer’s firm, which we will call RockSolid Portfolio Partners. Understanding this policy will provide the foundation for the Schmidt-Miller IPS we develop in the following sections.

Investment Philosophy: RockSolid Portfolio Partners believes in evidence-based investing. The firm holds that markets are generally efficient but recognizes that certain factors - such as value, size, and profitability - have historically delivered premium returns that can be captured through systematic exposure. RockSolid combines low-cost passive vehicles for core market exposure with selective active strategies in areas where research suggests active management can add value, such as emerging market equities and high-yield bonds. The firm prioritizes after-tax returns and seeks to minimize unnecessary costs and tax drag.

Asset Allocation Framework: RockSolid considers global equities, developed and emerging market bonds, real estate investment trusts, and commodities as the core building blocks of client portfolios. The firm uses strategic asset allocation with client-specific adjustments based on risk tolerance, time horizon, and liquidity needs. For a client with moderate risk tolerance, the neutral allocation might be 50 percent equities, 35 percent fixed income, and 15 percent real assets, with permissible ranges that allow for tactical adjustments.

Risk Management Principles: RockSolid limits exposure to any single security to five percent of the portfolio and any single sector to twenty-five percent. The firm monitors portfolios continuously and rebalances whenever allocations deviate by more than five percentage points from targets. In periods of extreme market volatility, the firm may implement temporary measures to protect client portfolios, but only within the bounds of the investment policy and with appropriate client communication.

ESG Considerations: RockSolid offers ESG-integrated portfolios for clients who request them. For these clients, the firm applies negative screens to exclude companies involved in controversial industries such as tobacco, firearms, and thermal coal. It also applies positive screens to favor companies with strong environmental and social practices, using data from leading ESG research providers. For clients who do not request ESG integration, Alpine considers material ESG factors as part of its fundamental research but does not apply systematic screens.

This firm-level policy provides the foundation for Elara to craft a tailored IPS for the Schmidt-Miller family. In the next section, we will explore the components of a client-specific Investment Policy Statement and begin developing one for Klaus, Elke, and Lukas, applying Alpine’s principles to their unique circumstances.

5.2.3 How Investment Policies Attract Clients

A well-articulated investment policy does more than guide internal decisions, it also signals to potential clients what the firm stands for and who it serves. RockSolid Partners’ policy, with its focus on evidence-based investing, global diversification, and disciplined risk management, naturally attracts clients who value these principles. Klaus and Elke Schmidt-Miller, for instance, were drawn to the firm precisely because its conservative approach aligned with Klaus’s need for capital preservation and Elke’s desire for thoughtful, values-driven management.

Transparent policies also build trust by setting clear expectations. When clients understand the firm’s investment philosophy, they are less likely to be surprised by market volatility or disappointed by short-term performance. This clarity fosters the kind of long-term relationships that benefit both clients and advisors. Moreover, a well-documented policy demonstrates regulatory compliance and provides a defensible framework for investment decisions, protecting both the firm and its clients.

5.3 Client-Specific Investment Policy Statement

With the firm-level investment policy of RockSolid Portfolio Partners established, we now turn to the central task of this chapter: crafting a client-specific Investment Policy Statement for the Schmidt-Miller family. Where the firm-level policy provides the philosophical foundation and strategic framework, the client-specific IPS applies that framework to the unique circumstances of Klaus, Elke, and Lukas. It translates everything we learned so far about the Schmidt-Miller family into a actionable document that will guide every investment decision made on their behalf.

5.3.1 What Is an Investment Policy Statement?

An Investment Policy Statement is a formal written document that outlines the investment objectives, constraints, and strategies for managing a specific client’s portfolio. It serves as reference for all investment decisions, ensuring that every trade, every allocation adjustment, and every rebalancing decision is made in accordance with the client’s expressed wishes and the advisor’s professional judgment.

The IPS is many things at once. It is a roadmap, charting the course from the client’s current financial position to their desired destination. It is a contract, documenting the agreement between client and advisor about how the portfolio will be managed and what each party can expect from the relationship. It is a behavioral guardrail, providing discipline when emotions threaten to derail the long-term plan. And it is a living document, evolving as the client’s circumstances change and as markets deliver their inevitable surprises.

For the Schmidt-Miller family, the IPS will need to address the complexity of a multi-generational situation with multiple goals, distinct risk profiles, and significant tax considerations. It must integrate Klaus’s need for capital preservation while still generating the returns necessary to fund their retirement. It must respect Elke’s emotional attachment to her inherited portfolio while building a diversified strategy. And it must provide clear guidance for Lukas’s separate financial journey, ensuring that his speculative instincts do not jeopardize his genuine needs.

5.3.2 Structure of an Investment Policy Statement

While IPS documents vary across firms and clients, most follow a similar structure that ensures all essential elements are addressed. A comprehensive IPS typically includes the following sections:

Section 1: Client Identification and Background

This section records basic identifying information about the client, including names, contact details, and a brief summary of their personal and financial circumstances. For the Schmidt-Miller family, this would include Klaus and Elke as the primary clients, with Lukas identified as a related party whose financial situation intersects with the family’s planning. It would also note key facts from the client analysis: Klaus’s imminent retirement from his medical practice, Elke’s inherited portfolio and her art gallery aspirations, and Lukas’s startup equity and down payment goal.

Section 2: Investment Objectives

This section articulates the client’s financial goals in specific, measurable terms. Rather than vague aspirations like “financial security,” the IPS should state concrete objectives such as “generate €160,000 in annual after-tax income, indexed for inflation, to fund retirement” or “accumulate €500,000 within 12 months for the establishment of an art gallery.” Each objective should be accompanied by its time horizon, priority level, and any relevant assumptions about inflation or taxes.

Section 3: Investment Constraints

This section documents the various factors that limit or constrain the investment strategy. These typically include:

  • Time Horizon: The expected duration of the investment period, which may differ for different goals. Klaus and Elke have a long-term horizon of 30-plus years for their retirement portfolio, but the gallery funding has a 12-month horizon and the down payment gift has a 3-to-5-year horizon.
  • Liquidity Requirements: The need for ready access to cash. The Schmidt-Millers require immediate liquidity for annual retirement withdrawals and near-term liquidity for the gallery and down payment.
  • Tax Considerations: The client’s tax situation and any strategies for tax-efficient investing. For Elke, the low-basis inherited portfolio creates a significant tax constraint that must be managed through careful harvesting and asset location decisions.
  • Legal and Regulatory Constraints: Any laws or regulations that affect investment choices. For the Schmidt-Millers, this includes the tax treatment of their Swiss residence and any cross-border considerations related to Lukas’s Berlin residency.
  • Unique Circumstances: Any other factors that bear on investment decisions. For Elke, this includes her endowment bias and emotional attachment to inherited assets. For Klaus, it includes his extreme loss aversion. For Lukas, it includes his speculative startup equity.

Section 4: Risk Tolerance

This section documents the client’s willingness and ability to bear risk, drawing on the quantitative and qualitative assessments conducted during the client analysis. It should distinguish between the client’s objective capacity for risk and their subjective comfort with volatility, and it should specify how any mismatches will be resolved. For Klaus, whose high ability to take risk is offset by his low willingness, the IPS must clearly state that the portfolio will be managed conservatively to respect his psychological need for security.

Section 5: Asset Allocation and Investment Strategy

This section establishes the principles and governance framework that will guide asset allocation decisions. It does not specify the actual allocation percentages, those are determined through a separate strategic asset allocation process and documented in a companion Investment Strategy Summary. Instead, this section focuses on the rules and guidelines that will govern how allocation decisions are made and maintained.

Key elements include:

  • Asset Allocation Principles: The core beliefs that will guide allocation decisions, such as diversification, goal segmentation, respect for the client’s risk constraints, and tax efficiency.
  • Portfolio Segmentation: How the portfolio will be divided into distinct buckets aligned with each goal’s time horizon and liquidity requirements. For the Schmidt-Millers, this includes a liquidity bucket for the art gallery, medium-term buckets for the down payment and Lake Garda property, and a long-term bucket for retirement and legacy.
  • Permissible Asset Classes: The types of investments that may be used in portfolio construction, such as global equities, fixed income, real assets, and cash equivalents. This provides boundaries while allowing flexibility for the strategic asset allocation process.
  • Rebalancing Policy: The conditions under which the portfolio will be rebalanced to maintain target allocations, including review frequency, tolerance bands, and methods for implementation.
  • Investment Selection Criteria: Guidelines for evaluating and selecting individual securities or managers, consistent with the firm-level investment policy.

The specific strategic asset allocation targets, including the actual percentages allocated to each asset class, are determined through the process described in Chapter 7 and documented in a separate Investment Strategy Summary that accompanies this IPS. This separation ensures that the IPS remains a stable, long-term governance document while the allocation can evolve with changing market conditions and client circumstances.

Section 6: Monitoring and Reporting

This section specifies how the portfolio’s performance will be evaluated and how progress will be communicated to the client. It typically includes:

  • Performance Benchmarks: Appropriate benchmarks for each asset class and for the portfolio as a whole. For a balanced portfolio, this might be a blended benchmark reflecting the target allocation.
  • Review Schedule: The frequency of formal portfolio reviews, typically quarterly or semi-annually, with a comprehensive annual review.
  • Reporting Requirements: The content and format of reports provided to the client, including performance data, fee disclosures, and commentary on significant events.

Section 7: Responsibilities

This section clarifies the roles and responsibilities of all parties involved in the investment process. It typically specifies:

  • Client Responsibilities: Providing accurate information, communicating changes in circumstances, and adhering to the agreed-upon plan.
  • Advisor Responsibilities: Managing the portfolio in accordance with the IPS, providing timely reporting, and communicating material developments.
  • Custodian and Other Service Provider Responsibilities: Holding assets safely, executing trades, and providing accurate record-keeping.

Section 8: Review and Amendment Procedures

This section establishes how the IPS will be maintained as a living document. It specifies the circumstances under which the IPS should be reviewed and the process for making amendments. It also documents that any changes to the investment strategy must be agreed upon by both parties and reflected in an updated IPS.

5.3.3 The IPS as a Behavioral Guardrail

One of the most important yet often overlooked functions of the IPS is its role as a behavioral guardrail. As we learned in Chapter 4, even the most sophisticated clients are subject to cognitive and emotional biases that can lead to poor investment decisions. Loss aversion causes investors to panic-sell during downturns. Overconfidence leads to excessive trading and inadequate diversification. Endowment bias prevents rational decisions about inherited assets.

A well-crafted IPS anticipates these biases and builds in safeguards that protect clients from themselves. For Klaus, whose loss aversion makes him prone to selling during market declines, the IPS can specify that the portfolio will be rebalanced according to a predetermined schedule rather than in response to market movements. It can also require that any major changes to the investment strategy be discussed during a scheduled review rather than made impulsively.

For Elke, whose endowment bias makes her reluctant to sell inherited assets, the IPS can explicitly acknowledge this attachment and establish a plan for gradually diversifying the portfolio over time, perhaps by committing to sell a certain percentage each year regardless of market conditions.

For Lukas, whose overconfidence might lead him to take excessive risks with his down payment funds, the IPS can clearly separate his speculative startup equity from his conservative down payment savings, establishing different investment policies for different goals. The document can also require that any changes to the down payment strategy be discussed with his parents and advisor, adding a layer of oversight to his natural impulsivity.

By documenting the agreed-upon strategy in advance, the IPS creates a commitment device that helps clients stay the course when emotions run high. When markets are falling and Klaus feels the urge to sell, he can look at the IPS and remember that this volatility was anticipated and that the plan calls for staying invested. When Elke struggles with the decision to sell a portion of her inherited portfolio, she can refer to the IPS and see that this sale is part of a thoughtful, long-term strategy rather than a betrayal of her parents’ memory.

5.3.4 The IPS as a Living Document

An Investment Policy Statement is not a static document. Instead it is a living document that should evolve as the client’s circumstances change and as markets deliver their inevitable surprises.

Most IPS documents specify a formal review schedule, typically annually, during which the advisor and client assess whether the investment strategy remains appropriate given any changes in the client’s life or financial situation. These reviews also provide an opportunity to evaluate portfolio performance against benchmarks and to discuss any adjustments that may be needed.

Beyond scheduled reviews, certain trigger events should prompt an immediate review of the IPS. These include:

  • Significant changes in the client’s personal circumstances, such as marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a spouse, or a major health event.
  • Significant changes in the client’s financial circumstances, such as an inheritance, a business sale, a career change, or a large unexpected expense.
  • Significant changes in market conditions or the economic outlook that might warrant a strategic adjustment.
  • Significant changes in tax laws or regulations that affect the investment strategy.
  • A material change in the client’s risk tolerance, as evidenced by their reaction to market events.

When such events occur, the advisor and client should review the IPS together, determine whether any adjustments are needed, and document any changes in a revised version of the document. This process ensures that the IPS remains aligned with the client’s current situation and that both parties maintain a shared understanding of the investment strategy.

5.4 Drafting the Schmidt-Miller IPS

With a clear understanding of the IPS structure and purpose, we now begin the practical work of drafting a comprehensive Investment Policy Statement for the Schmidt-Miller family. This section focuses on the foundational elements of any IPS: a clear statement of the client’s investment objectives and the constraints within which the portfolio must be managed. These elements flow directly from the client analysis conducted in Chapter 4 and must be documented with precision to ensure that all subsequent investment decisions align with the family’s true needs.

5.4.1 Client Identification and Background

Every IPS begins with a clear identification of the client and a brief summary of their relevant personal and financial circumstances. This section serves both as a reference for the advisor and as documentation that the advisor has conducted a thorough inquiry into the client’s situation.

For the Schmidt-Miller family, this section would read as follows:

Klaus Schmidt-Miller is a orthopaedic surgeon who has practiced for over three decades. He is in the process of selling his stake in the surgical practice he co-founded, which will result in after-tax proceeds of approximately €18 million within the next 60 days. Elke recently inherited a diversified portfolio of stocks and bonds from her parents, valued at €2.5 million. This portfolio has a very low cost basis, creating significant unrealized capital gains.

5.4.1.3 Investment Constraints

The investment constraints section documents the various factors that limit or shape the investment strategy. These constraints arise from the client’s personal circumstances, the legal and regulatory environment, and the firm’s policies. Together with the investment objectives, they define the boundaries within which the portfolio must be constructed.

For the Schmidt-Miller family, Elara has identified the following constraints:

Time Horizon

The Schmidt-Miller family presents multiple time horizons corresponding to their different goals:

Liquidity Requirements

The family has significant and immediate liquidity needs:

  • Annual retirement withdrawals of €160,000, requiring that the first year’s spending be held in cash or very short-term instruments.
  • €500,000 for the art gallery, needed in approximately 12 months.
  • €400,000 for Lukas’s down payment, with the first portion potentially needed within 3 years.
  • €2 million for the Lake Garda property, needed in 5 years.

These requirements dictate that the portfolio cannot be invested as a single monolithic block. Instead, funds must be segmented into distinct buckets, each with its own investment approach aligned with its time horizon and liquidity need.

Tax Considerations

The family faces several significant tax considerations:

  • Klaus’s €18 million sale proceeds are after-tax, meaning no embedded capital gain liability exists for these funds.
  • Elke’s inherited portfolio of €2.5 million has a very low cost basis, creating substantial unrealized capital gains. Selling these assets would trigger tax at the 20 percent capital gains rate.
  • Annual portfolio income will be taxed at progressive rates up to 40 percent. Investment decisions should consider the tax efficiency of different asset classes and structures.
  • Klaus’s retirement account of €800,000 is tax-advantaged; withdrawals will be taxed as ordinary income, but the account grows tax-free in the meantime.
  • The family resides in Switzerland, which may impose annual wealth tax in some cantons.

These tax considerations will influence asset location decisions (which assets are held in taxable versus tax-advantaged accounts), the timing of sales from Elke’s inherited portfolio, and the selection of tax-efficient investment vehicles.

Legal and Regulatory Constraints

  • The family is subject to Swiss tax and regulatory requirements, which must be considered in all investment decisions.
  • Lukas’s residence in Berlin introduces cross-border considerations for any gifts or transfers, which should be reviewed with tax specialists.
  • All investments must comply with applicable securities laws and regulations in Switzerland and any other relevant jurisdictions.

Unique Circumstances and Behavioral Considerations

The client analysis revealed several unique circumstances that constrain the investment strategy:

  • Klaus’s Loss Aversion: Klaus views the €18 million as the hard-won fruit of a lifetime of labor and is extremely averse to losses. Any strategy that exposes significant principal to market volatility will cause him anxiety and may lead to poor decisions during downturns. The portfolio must be structured to minimize the frequency and severity of losses, even at the cost of lower expected returns.
  • Elke’s Endowment Bias: Elke feels a strong emotional connection to her inherited portfolio and is reluctant to sell these assets, even when diversification would be prudent. The investment strategy must accommodate this attachment while still building a diversified overall portfolio. This may involve keeping a core holding of inherited stocks while diversifying around them.
  • Lukas’s Overconfidence and Separate Goals: Lukas’s speculative startup equity is separate from the family’s core wealth and should be treated as such. His down payment funds, however, must be managed conservatively despite his personal risk tolerance. These funds should be held separately and invested according to their own short-term horizon.
  • Elke’s Art Gallery: The gallery is a passion project, not a financial investment. The €500,000 allocated to this goal should be treated as an expense, not as part of the investment portfolio, and its expected return is zero.

5.4.1.5 Return Requirements

Core Retirement Portfolio

After setting aside funds for the specific shorter-term goals, approximately €18.55 million remains for the long-term retirement and legacy portfolio. This portfolio must:

  1. Generate €160,000 in annual after-tax income, indexed for inflation, for Klaus and Elke’s lifetime.
  2. Preserve the real value of the remaining principal for legacy transfer to Lukas and future generations.

The simple spending rate is €160,000 / €18,550,000 = 0.86%. However, this naive calculation understates the true return requirement because it ignores:

  • Inflation: Assuming 2.5% average inflation, the withdrawal in year 10 will need to be approximately €205,000 to maintain the same purchasing power.
  • Taxes: Withdrawals and portfolio income will be taxed at rates up to 40%. To have €160,000 after tax, the portfolio must earn significantly more before tax.
  • Legacy Preservation: To maintain the real value of the principal, the portfolio must earn returns that at least keep pace with inflation after withdrawals and taxes.

A more realistic analysis suggests that the portfolio needs to earn a real after-tax return of approximately 4% to sustainably fund the desired spending while preserving the real value of the legacy. This translates to a nominal pre-tax return in the range of 6-8% , depending on inflation and the tax efficiency of portfolio construction.

Short-Term and Medium-Term Buckets

The funds allocated to specific shorter-term goals have different return requirements:

Goal Amount Horizon Return Requirement Investment Approach
Art Gallery €500,000 1 year Near-zero; capital preservation paramount Cash or very short-term bonds
Down Payment €400,000 3-5 years Modest; preservation priority Short-term bond ladder or conservative balanced fund
Lake Garda €2,000,000 5 years Moderate; some growth needed to offset inflation but preservation important Medium-term bond ladder with modest equity allocation if flexibility exists

Feasibility Assessment

A nominal pre-tax return target of 6-8% for the core portfolio is realistic but not guaranteed. Historical returns on a diversified portfolio of global equities and bonds have averaged in this range over long periods, but with significant variability. A portfolio constructed to achieve this target will likely have an equity allocation of 40-60%, which implies potential peak-to-trough losses of 20-30% in severe bear markets.

This level of volatility is at the upper limit of what Klaus’s loss aversion can tolerate. The portfolio must be structured to minimize the frequency and severity of losses while still pursuing the required return. This may involve:

  • A higher allocation to high-quality bonds than a purely mathematical optimization would suggest.
  • Use of structured products or other strategies that provide downside protection.
  • A dedicated income bucket that provides psychological comfort even if not mathematically optimal.

5.4.1.6 Asset Allocation and Investment Strategy Framework

With the client’s objectives, constraints, risk tolerance, and return requirements clearly documented, we now establish the governance framework that will guide asset allocation and investment decisions. This section does not specify the actual allocation percentages, those are determined through the strategic asset allocation process described in Chapter 7 and documented in a separate Investment Strategy Summary. Instead, it establishes the principles, boundaries, and procedures that will govern how allocation decisions are made and maintained over time.

Asset Allocation Principles

The Schmidt-Miller portfolio will be constructed according to the following principles:

  • Diversification: The portfolio will be diversified across multiple asset classes to reduce reliance on any single source of return and to manage overall portfolio volatility. Diversification recognizes that different asset classes perform differently under varying economic conditions and that a balanced portfolio can achieve more consistent returns than any single asset class alone.
  • Goal Segmentation: Funds will be allocated to distinct sub-portfolios or “buckets” aligned with each financial goal’s time horizon and liquidity requirements. Short-term goals will be funded with capital preservation instruments, while long-term goals may include growth-oriented assets. This segmentation ensures that short-term needs do not compromise long-term strategy.
  • Respect for the Binding Constraint: Klaus’s low risk tolerance is the binding constraint for the core retirement portfolio. All allocation decisions will respect this constraint, even if it implies lower expected returns. The portfolio will be structured to minimize the frequency and severity of losses to ensure that Klaus can adhere to the plan through market cycles.
  • Tax Efficiency: Asset location decisions will be made with careful attention to tax efficiency. Tax-inefficient assets such as taxable bonds will be prioritized for Klaus’s retirement account, while tax-efficient assets such as equities will be prioritized for taxable accounts. Sales from Elke’s inherited portfolio will be managed over time to control tax impact.
  • Cost Awareness: All investment decisions will consider the impact of costs on net returns. Where appropriate, low-cost passive vehicles such as index funds and ETFs will be used to gain core market exposure. Active strategies will be considered only in areas where research suggests the potential for excess returns sufficient to justify higher fees.
  • Behavioral Accommodation: The portfolio will be structured to accommodate the behavioral biases identified during client analysis, as detailed in the Unique Circumstances section above.

Based on the family’s multiple goals with different time horizons, the portfolio will be segmented as follows:

Permissible Asset Classes

The following asset classes are considered suitable for the Schmidt-Miller portfolio, subject to the strategic asset allocation process and the client’s risk constraints:

  • Developed Market Equities: Swiss, European, North American, Japan
  • Emerging Market Equities: Selected markets with appropriate risk controls
  • Government Bonds: Swiss and developed market government bonds
  • Investment-Grade Corporate Bonds: Diversified across issuers and sectors
  • Inflation-Linked Bonds: For inflation protection
  • Cash and Cash Equivalents: Money market funds, short-term government bonds, bank deposits

Excluded Asset Classes

The following asset classes are not considered suitable for this portfolio:

  • Private equity and hedge funds (due to liquidity constraints and complexity)
  • Cryptocurrencies and other speculative digital assets
  • Structured products with opaque fee structures or inappropriate risk characteristics

Rebalancing Policy

The portfolio will be rebalanced to maintain target allocations using the following guidelines:

  • Review Frequency: The portfolio will be reviewed quarterly for rebalancing needs.
  • Tolerance Bands: Rebalancing will be triggered when any asset class deviates from its target allocation by more than five percentage points.
  • Method: Rebalancing will be accomplished through a combination of directing new cash flows to underweight asset classes and, when necessary, selling overweight positions.
  • Tax Awareness: Rebalancing decisions will consider tax implications, with a preference for using new contributions and dividend reinvestments to adjust allocations in taxable accounts.

Investment Selection Criteria

All investments selected for the Schmidt-Miller portfolio will be evaluated against the following criteria:

  • Passive Investments (Index Funds and ETFs): Low expense ratio relative to category peers; tracking error consistent with the stated index; sufficient assets under management and trading liquidity; provider reputation and operational stability.
  • Active Investments (Selected Strategies): Demonstrated performance relative to benchmark over a full market cycle; consistent investment process that is well understood; stable and experienced investment team; fees that are reasonable given the strategy; alignment of interests between manager and investors.
  • Fixed Income Investments: Credit quality appropriate to the role in the portfolio; duration consistent with the strategic allocation; for individual bonds, diversification across issuers and sectors.
  • Elke’s Inherited Holdings: A core holding of the most emotionally significant inherited stocks may be maintained, subject to a maximum concentration limit of 5% of total portfolio value and regular review to ensure continued suitability. The remaining inherited assets will be gradually diversified over time according to a schedule documented in the Investment Strategy Summary.

5.5 Conclusion

In summary, a well-defined investment policy is vital for the effective management of client portfolios and the overall success of the investment firm. It ensures operational consistency, risk management, regulatory compliance, and client satisfaction. Regular reviews and adaptations of the investment policy are necessary to keep pace with changes in market conditions, regulatory environments, and client requirements. By prioritizing a robust investment policy, investment firms can enhance their strategic decision-making, maintain high standards of client service, and achieve sustained success in the competitive world of investment management.
